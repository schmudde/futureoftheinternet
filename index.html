How They Broke the Internet (and How We Can Fix It)

The United States has gone from Internet pioneer to a country facing a crisis of conscious, where a lack political will and few consumer options provide substandard service at high prices. The February 26th vote on Net Neutrality is an important step towards a truly free and open Internet, but there is more to be done.

I. Building the Internet

The Public Network

1969

ARPANET, the first nationwide network, is created in 1969. Similar infrastructure will be built (primarily with taxpayer money) for the next ~20 years and evolve into today's Internet.

The Internet Goes Private

1995

The final step in a long process, the last government-run Internet backbone, NSFNET, was shut down.

The Internet in the United States has been privately owned & operated ever since.

The Social Contract

1995-2000

FCC allowed Time Warner and Comcast to raise rates up to $5/month, on the promise the revenue would be funneled directly into deploying fiber optic technology, wiring schools, & increasing reliability.

States & Phone Companies Broadband Plan

1992-2005

Telecommunication companies lobbied for "alternative regulations" that allowed them to charge customers extra for explicit investment into broadband deployment.

The Result

2015

The country that invented the Internet has dropped from offering the fastest Internet in the world to 9th.

II. Consolidating the Internet

Common Carrier Established

1934

Eventually known as Title II, this ensured that every American had affordable telephone service.

All privately-owned cable built under Title II must be open to any service provider, enhancing competition. Installations are usually conducted as a private-public partnership as a result.

Title II Broadband Cables Reclassified as Title I

2005

Although many telecommunications companies built Title II broadband infrastructure utilizing publicly-financed incentives, the sudden FCC action drove down competition and de-incentivized rollouts to poor and rural areas.

The Number of ISPs Decrease

Late 1990s

Households in the United States could once choose from several ISPs. Today's home is lucky to have more than one ISP option.

The Result

2015

67% of Americans have no more than two broadband options.

Nearly 30% of Americans have no option at all.

III. The Decline of the Internet

Failure: The Social Contract

1995-2000

No evidence exists that the cable companies ever wired schools and libraries. No evidence exists that the temporary rate hike was ever reduced.

Failure: State & Phone Company Broadband Deployment

1993-2010

Example: In exchange for new utility customer surcharges & loosened regulation, Verizon promised to deliver 45 Mbps fiber service to all the households in New Jersey.

As of today, they haven't provided that service to even half the state.

The Result

2015

Incredible profits for Internet service providers and a lost decade of fiber deployment from 1992-2005.

The cost per megabyte of data in America ($0.53) is 10x what it is in Japan ($0.04) for slower speeds.

IV. The Future of the Internet

US Congress

US Courts

We Need: Net Neutrality

The FCC's vote on Net Neutrality is important, but we're likely to see opposition mounted in the courts and in Congress.

FCC

We Need: Open Networks

Open up the wires and allow free market competition, from the hub to the home.

State & Federal Government

We Need: To Audit the ISPs

We haven't gotten what they paid for. In fact, we may still be paying for it. It's time for our state representatives to step up and audit the Internet Service Providers.
